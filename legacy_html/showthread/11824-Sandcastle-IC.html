    Sandcastle.


    The moon has made place for it's other half.
    It shines ever so brightly upon the city of Rayt.
    Life wakes slowly, but surely.
    It's a small world that never changes.
    You open your eyes on the same spot every morning.
    You see the same people, time after time.
    A man can't truly escape his roots.
    He will always return to the place of his birth.
    But the people of Rayt are at peace with the familiar.
    Only a select few individuals strive for change, freedom and the unknown.



    I close the door behind me. The empty streets welcome me and the clouds seem to flee from my sight, as if they fear my arrival. I can see the people who watch me, and every single one of my movements closely. They stare from behind their casings, but it's silent inside the sand-coloured buildings. I want to avoid the drowsy gazes; so I try to leave the houses behind me as soon as I can. The people can see me, but most don't know me. They don't know my name, some don't even know my voice. To them, I am who the person they see, a vivid and self-assured woman passing through the streets at a steady place.

    When this world is lit by the naked sun, the revealed land is somewhat familiar to me, but scary nonetheless. Do I want to go back to the past? No. Probably. Yes. But this present is inescapable and everyone needs to face the music one day. My previous life will continue to linger deep inside of me for a while; casting it away is too difficult. That would be like breaking myself into pieces and I'd rather be a scarred, whole person than a broken one. I am Shula also.

    I dare to step in parts of the city I only vaguely remember, which I consider very brave of myself. Shula would never set foot in such a neighbourhood and that's the proof I have the upper hand. She stares through my eyes, but doesn't have a word to protest with. I can feel her shiver with every step I take, my own body remains calm and gracefully moves forward. Shula clamps on to me, Lamya, a person who is largely unfamiliar to her, but she trusts her and silently accepts everything she does.

    It has been seven years since I lived in this world. I was a sixteen-year-old girl when I left and now am a full-grown woman, twenty-three of age. I've picked the locks of her shackles and am free to go. I just wish Shula's lashes didn't show on my arms. But where can I find a new canvas?

    The sun was already bright enough to narrow the eyes as it passed across the dusty floor through the narrow slit that was the room's only window. Russet gaze snapping open, Khalidah dropped it to settle upon the small object in her tanned palm, a slow smile creeping across her full lips. Tossing the fruit into the air, the dancer caught it deftly, her feet already taking her to the sandy frame that overlooked the dirty streets of Rayt as she bit into the crisp flesh. Few people were out at this hour - here and there a tiny rodent skittered across the sandy paths - and the morning birds twittered raucously as the hot, glowing orb made its way back up the clear skies.

    Squinting into the light, Khalidah reached up with her free hand and lowered the cloak-like burnoose from her head, running her slender fingers through her tangled, sand-encrusted locks in an attempt to loosen the knots within the dark curls. The effort - as always - did nothing and, with a sigh, Khalidah turned from the window, tossing the hard pit from her stolen fruit out onto the street below. Crouching, the young woman rummaged through an old sack hidden unobtrusively in the corner, her slim hand emerging a few moments later with a comb made of bone, many of the teeth broken and jagged but still nonetheless usable. Dragging the remaining teeth through her hair, Khalidah returned to her observations of the street, dark gaze flashing quickly over the few people below as she thought.

    Eventually, after some moment of contemplation, her ebony curls were untangled once more and tossing the comb back to its place, Khalidah wrapped the teal-coloured cloth around her face once more. Nothing had changed. Rayt was still its old, dusty self and she... she was no more than a thief and a rogue. Every day began the same and it always would.

    The sand stirred upon the stone floor as the cloaked shadow left without a sound.

    Nothing had changed.

The most Matta had managed to acchieve was the planting of the tree under which he lay, unfortunately it had been positioned wrongly so, unless he woke up early, he would be blinded every morning by the sunlight seeping round the sides of the tree.

    Pushing himself up, his hand slipping slightly and digging into the sand, Matta began the 20 minute journey that would lead him to his deposit box, which he rented out in one of the cheaper Inns near the Oasis. He never told the Innkeeper why he visited the box every morning, suspecting that the man's prices would rise considerably should he find out where his customer woke up each day. Matta arrived eventually at the Inn and, fishing his set of keys out from around his neck, he wordlessly showed one to the Innkeeper who went to unlock the door to the deposit room, knowing that striking up a conversation with Matta this early in the morning wouldn't get him very far.

    Once the door had been unlocked Matta entered, coming out 30 seconds later with some money so that he could fill his gourd with water. Once this was accomplished he went back inside to unpack his gear from the large reddish-brown trunk in which he stored al his worldly posessions. From the box he extracted an expensive looking buzuq and his kaskara, there was no need to travel heavy today so he left it at that, figuring that he would be able to find somewhere to eat later in the day. He relocked the box, and, fishing out some more coins, passed them to the innkeeper and ordered a breakfast.

    Cowl billowing lightly around her curved form in the warm breeze, Khalidah watched the streets of Rayt from the pleasant cool of the tavern's shade, back resting gently upon the rough sandstone and arms folded loosely. In the haze of heat already radiating from the golden ground, the large tawny shape of the temple loomed - almost looking ominous with the huge circular chasm of the entrance as a gaping mouth and sandy steps like jagged teeth - a few people hurrying to pay their daily tribute. Huffing once through her nose, Khalidah shoved herself away from the wall, dark eyes narrowed in the shadowed slit of her burnoose. Fools. The only way a man - or woman - ever prospered in life was solely by their own means and wit. To rely on the benevolence of beings that are invisible and intangible was simply nothing more than foolishness. Look where it had got her.

    Her feet, as always, were making their own way across the city, taking her inconspicuously through the crowds as she had done every day since she had relied on no-one but herself to see her through the day. Dust swirled around her tarnished gold sandles, coating her tanned feet in a comforting layer of fine sand grains, yet she paid no mind, her eyes sweeping the busy market and her ears continually pricked for any piece of useful information they may happen upon. You never knew when your luck would turn.

    A small movement caught her eye - a glint of steel on the edge of her peripheral vision - and Khalidah turned her head to investigate, noticing the darkened figure crouched inconspicuously at the bottom of the market steps. Metal swung lazily over the ground - almost hypnotising in its regularity - and before she could stop herself, she was making her way over. Even more of a curiosity was that the strange figure was a child - barely at the cusp of adulthood, no less.

    Cocking her head to one side, Khalidah observed the girl from behind for a short while, her slender arms folded beneath her cloak. Eventually, the child paused and the young woman took the brief moment to speak. "

    You should not be out on the streets this early, child," she said in an undertone and though her mouth didn't move, her eyes showed more than simple curiosity, "It isn't safe. Go claim sanctuary at the temple, if you must, though little good that will do you." The latter she muttered in an undertone, shrouded face turning automatically to survey the building mentioned, for as much as she disliked the place, no-one could say that it wasn't safe.

    Absently, Khalidah rummaged at her belt, procuring a single copper coin and tossing it to the floor before the girl, meeting her blue-ish gaze with a disarming smile. "The baker over there will sell you a loaf for that," she told the child, flashing a wink, "and if he doesn't, well, then more fool him: return to me and I'll get you something else instead."
